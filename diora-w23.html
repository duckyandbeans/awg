Part I: The Sonnet

📖 About This Poem

Type & Author: This is a Shakespearean (English) Sonnet written by William Shakespeare. It consists of 14 lines written in iambic pentameter.

Rhyme Scheme: It follows a strict ABAB CDCD EFEF GG pattern. The poem ends with a "rhyming couplet" (the last two lines), which usually provides a thematic twist or a final summary of the poem’s message.

Part II: The Narrative Ballad

📖 About This Poem

Type & Author: This is a Narrative Ballad by Edgar Allan Poe. Ballads are meant to tell a story and often have a musical, rhythmic quality that makes them feel like a song or a folk legend.

Rhyme Scheme: Poe uses a varied but repetitive scheme, primarily ABABCB. Notice how he consistently returns to the "ee" sound (sea, Lee, me) throughout the stanzas to create a haunting, melodic effect that emphasizes the setting and the subject.

Part III: The Rhymed Verse

📖 About This Poem

Type & Author: This is a classic Lyric Poem by Robert Frost. It is famous for its "Chain Rhyme" structure and its meditative, calm tone.

Rhyme Scheme: Frost uses a sophisticated AABA BCBB CDCC DDDD scheme. In each stanza, the third line introduces the rhyme for the next stanza, creating a "pulling" effect that mimics the traveler moving through the woods, until the final stanza where every line rhymes to bring the poem to a complete stop.

Part IV: The Poet's Desk

📖 The Anatomy of a Sonnet

  • 14 Lines: A sonnet always has exactly fourteen lines.
  • Rhyme Scheme: ABAB CDCD EFEF GG. It is broken into three quatrains (4 lines each) and ends with a couplet (2 lines).
  • Iambic Pentameter: The rhythm of a heartbeat! Each line typically has 10 syllables with a bouncing "da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM" beat.
  • The Volta (Turn): Usually around line 9 (or sometimes at the final couplet), the poem shifts its tone, answers a question, or reveals a twist!

Part V: The Critic's Chair

📖 What Makes a Poem "Bad"?

  • Forced Rhymes: Choosing words just because they rhyme, even if they make no sense (e.g., rhyming "love" with "shove" when it doesn't fit).
  • Broken Meter: A rhythm that is clunky, skips beats, or forces the reader to stumble over too many or too few syllables.
  • Clichés & Melodrama: Overused phrases ("roses are red") and emotions that are extremely exaggerated.
