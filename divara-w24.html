Present perfect 1

Present perfect

Form Affirmative form Negative form Interrogative form & short answer
I / you / we / they have ('ve) taken the book. I / you / we / they have not (haven't) taken the book. Have I / you / we / they taken the book?
Yes, I / you / we / they have.
No, I / you / we / they haven't.
He / she / it has ('s) taken the book. He / she / it has not (hasn't) taken the book. Has he / she / it taken the book?
Yes, he / she / it has.
No, he / she / it hasn't.
Watch out!
To form the present perfect, we use the verb to have in the present tense and the past participle of the main verb. The past participle of regular verbs ends in -ed. The past participle forms of irregular verbs must be learned by heart.

Irregular Verbs (Examples):
be → been | become → become | begin → begun | buy → bought | come → come | do → done | give → given | know → known | make → made | put → put | read → read | speak → spoken | take → taken | teach → taught | understand → understood | write → written
Used for describing:Examples
actions without stating the (exact) time of their completion
the result of an action
questions about acquired experience in the past and answers to them		 I've finished my school history project.
David has lost his school bag.
Have you ever cheated in a test? No, I've never cheated in a test.
actions or states which started in the past and continue to the present moment We've been at school for 6 hours.
Mrs Clark has worked at this school since 2012.
Helpful hints
Present perfect is usually used with:
• yet: We haven't checked the answers yet.
• already: We have already done this exercise.
• just: We have just done this exercise.
• since (last week / 2013, etc.): Mr Gray has taught English here since 2013.
• for (a year / two days, etc.): She's taught French here for a year.
• ever: Have you ever had guitar lessons?
• never: I've never understood why they give us so much homework!

Present perfect 2

Present perfect and past simple

Tense Used for: Example
present perfect describing the result of a completed action She's taken photos for her geography project.
(She now has photos which she can use in her project.)
past simple describing a completed action in the past She took photos for her geography project.
(The action is complete, but the result is unknown to us. For example, we don't know if these photos exist now.)
present perfect describing an action, the completion time of which is unknown or unstated She's finished her geography project.
past simple describing an action, the completion time of which is known or stated She finished her geography project last night.
Helpful hints
There are time markers that hint at which grammatical tense should be used.

Past simple
• ago: Robert took his driving test a year ago.
• yesterday: We had our biology test yesterday.
• last week: Jack started guitar lessons last week.
• a few days ago: I saw the new documentary a few days ago.
• on Sunday: Tom did his English homework on Sunday.
• at eight o'clock: They arrived at school at eight o'clock.
• in 2013: She started teaching in 2013.

Present perfect
• already: We have already done this exercise.
• ever: Have you ever had guitar lessons?
• for: She has taught German here for over five years.
• just: We have just done this exercise.
• never: I have never understood why they give us so much homework.
• since: Mr Gray has taught French here since 2006.
• yet: We haven't checked the answers yet.
